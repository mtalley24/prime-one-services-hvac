Change Your Filters Every 1-3 Months

Your HVAC system's air filter is like the lungs of your home — it catches dust, pollen, and debris before it enters your living space. A clogged filter forces your system to work harder, using more energy and reducing cooling or heating efficiency. Check your filter monthly and replace it every 1-3 months depending on use, pets, and air quality. A simple filter change can save you money on energy bills and extend your system's lifespan.

Pro tip: Set a calendar reminder so you don't forget. Keeping filters fresh is one of the easiest and most important maintenance tasks you can do.

Keep Your Outdoor Unit Clear

Your air conditioner's outdoor condenser unit needs clear airflow to release heat efficiently. Make sure to trim back vegetation, remove leaves and debris, and keep the area around the unit clean. Avoid placing large objects near the unit and never block the top or sides. Proper airflow prevents your system from overheating and working inefficiently.

Regular cleaning (a gentle spray from a garden hose in spring and fall) helps maintain peak performance and prevents expensive repairs from heat stress.

Don't Close Too Many Vents

It's tempting to close vents in unused rooms to "save energy," but this actually backfires. When you block vents, you create pressure imbalances that force your HVAC system to work harder and can damage internal components. The system is designed to push air through all vents, and blocking them disrupts that balance.

Instead, keep vents open throughout your home. If you want to heat or cool certain areas more than others, use a programmable thermostat or zone control system designed for that purpose.

Schedule Annual Maintenance

Professional HVAC maintenance is like a health checkup for your system. A technician will inspect components, check refrigerant levels, test safety controls, clean coils, and identify potential problems before they become expensive repairs. Annual tune-ups in spring (before cooling) and fall (before heating) keep everything running smoothly.

Most homeowners who get annual maintenance spend far less on repairs and enjoy longer system life than those who wait for breakdowns. It's an investment that pays for itself through improved efficiency and reliability.

Check Your Thermostat Settings

Your thermostat controls comfort and energy costs. Make sure the temperature settings match your lifestyle — too hot in summer or too cold in winter wastes energy. A difference of just 7-10 degrees for 8 hours a day can save up to 10% on heating and cooling bills. During winter, set it lower when away or sleeping; in summer, set it higher when the house is empty.

If your thermostat is old and constantly being adjusted, consider upgrading to a smart or programmable model that learns your schedule and adjusts automatically.

Seal Duct Leaks

Leaky ductwork can cause you to lose 20-30% of your cooled or heated air before it reaches your rooms. This means your system works harder and your energy bills climb. Common leak spots are where ducts connect, at vents, and in attics or crawlspaces. Sealing leaks with mastic sealant or duct tape can dramatically improve efficiency.

A professional can perform a duct leakage test to identify problems. Proper sealing ensures the air your system produces actually reaches your living spaces, saving energy and improving comfort.

Use a Programmable Thermostat

Programmable and smart thermostats automatically adjust your temperature based on your schedule, so you're not heating or cooling an empty home. You can set different temperatures for weekdays, weekends, morning, and night. Smart models even learn your patterns and adjust automatically, giving you comfort without wasting energy.

Many utility companies offer rebates for upgrading to a programmable thermostat, and the savings on energy bills usually pay for the device within a year or two.

Know When to Replace vs Repair

If your HVAC system is over 15 years old, repair costs are mounting, and it needs frequent service, it might be time to replace rather than repair. A new high-efficiency system costs more upfront but uses less energy, runs more reliably, and qualifies for rebates or tax credits. Compare the cost of repair against replacement before deciding.

As a rule of thumb: if a repair costs more than half the price of a new system, replacement is usually the better investment. Let us know if you want a professional assessment — we can help you decide the best path forward for your home.
